# Satisficing (Simon)

**Type:** concept

Simon's principle of bounded rationality where agents achieve acceptable quality within resource limits rather than maximizing, directly operationalized by Agent Contract success criteria and resource bounds.

---

Satisficing is a principle of bounded rationality introduced by Herbert Simon that describes decision-making strategies where an agent seeks outcomes that are good enough rather than optimal, operating within real-world constraints on time, information, and computation. Simon argued that classical utility maximization is an unrealistic model for agents operating in complex environments, and that practical intelligence involves finding acceptable solutions efficiently. The Agent Contracts framework directly operationalizes satisficing through its success criteria component (Φ), which defines fulfillment as achieving a weighted threshold of conditions rather than perfect scores, and through its resource constraints (R), which bound the computation available for any task. This grounding in bounded rationality positions Agent Contracts within a broader tradition of cognitively and economically realistic models of intelligent behavior.
